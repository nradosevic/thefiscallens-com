India's Manufacturing Moment: Why Global Capital Is Taking Notice

By Vikram Mehta | February 25, 2026

India has long been discussed as a potential manufacturing powerhouse, yet for decades the country's industrial sector underperformed its potential while services and IT led economic growth. That dynamic is now shifting in ways that have captured the attention of global corporations and investors alike. A confluence of factors—including supply chain diversification pressures, favorable demographics, and ambitious government incentives—has created what many observers call India's most promising manufacturing opportunity in a generation.

The drive to reduce concentration in global supply chains, accelerated by pandemic disruptions and geopolitical tensions, has pushed multinational corporations to consider alternatives to established manufacturing hubs. India's position as a large, democratic country with relatively low labor costs and an improving business environment makes it a natural beneficiary of this "China plus one" strategy. Apple's expansion of iPhone assembly in India represents the most prominent example of this trend, but similar moves are occurring across electronics, pharmaceuticals, and automotive sectors.

Government policy has evolved to support manufacturing investment more effectively than in previous cycles. The Production Linked Incentive scheme offers substantial subsidies to companies that establish or expand manufacturing in India across fourteen targeted sectors. Infrastructure investment has improved logistics capabilities, reducing a historical competitive disadvantage. Regulatory reforms, while still incomplete, have simplified the process of operating manufacturing facilities. The combination of carrots and improved conditions has resonated with corporate decision-makers.

India's demographic profile offers structural advantages for manufacturing expansion. With a median age of 28 and a large population still transitioning from agriculture to industry, the country possesses the labor force required for manufacturing at scale. Unlike China, where the working-age population has begun declining, India will add workers for decades to come. However, skills gaps remain a challenge, requiring investment in vocational training to prepare workers for advanced manufacturing roles.

Foreign direct investment into India has surged, with manufacturing-focused flows reaching record levels. Global private equity firms and sovereign wealth funds have increased allocations to Indian manufacturing and industrial real estate. Domestic conglomerates are expanding capacity and forming joint ventures with technology partners. The pipeline of announced investments suggests momentum will continue, though execution timelines often extend beyond initial projections.

Challenges remain significant despite the optimism. Land acquisition complexities, inconsistent power supply in some regions, and bureaucratic hurdles continue to frustrate investors. Labor laws vary by state, creating a patchwork of regulatory requirements. Port and transportation infrastructure, while improving, still lags global benchmarks in efficiency. Companies succeeding in Indian manufacturing often cite the importance of local partnerships and patience in navigating the operating environment.

For portfolio investors, India's manufacturing renaissance presents multiple entry points. Listed industrial companies stand to benefit from the investment cycle, though valuations in many cases already reflect high expectations. Real estate investment trusts focused on industrial properties offer exposure to manufacturing growth without single-company risk. Infrastructure and logistics companies provide another avenue for participation. Understanding which segments are best positioned requires granular analysis of specific manufacturing subsectors and their competitive dynamics versus global alternatives.
